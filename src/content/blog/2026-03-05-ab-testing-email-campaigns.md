# A/B Testing Emails: What to Test, How Long to Wait, and How to Know You've Won

Everyone says you should A/B test, but without a clear framework, you'll waste time testing the wrong things and drawing false conclusions. Here's a battle‑tested approach.

## What to Test (in Priority Order)

1. **Subject line:** The biggest lever for open rates. Test benefit‑driven vs. curiosity‑driven, short vs. long, with and without personalization.
2. **Sender name:** “Company Name” vs. “[Name] from Company” can swing open rates by 5–15%.
3. **CTA copy and placement:** Button vs. text link, “Get Started” vs. “See Plans”, top vs. bottom placement.
4. **Send time:** Day of week and time of day. Even small shifts can make a measurable difference.
5. **Email copy length:** Short vs. long form. Some audiences want a quick hit; others need details.

## How Long to Run a Test

Most email opens happen within 24 hours. For campaigns, a 24‑48 hour testing window is sufficient. For automated flows, let it run for at least one full cycle (e.g., 7 days for a welcome sequence) to get statistically significant data.

**Rule of thumb:** Wait until the losing variant has at least 100 opens or clicks before calling a winner. Small sample sizes produce misleading results.

## Statistical Significance (Without the Math)

Many email platforms have built‑in confidence calculations. If yours doesn't, a simple rule: if the difference between variants is under 5%, you likely need more data. A 10%+ difference with a few hundred recipients is usually reliable.

## What Not to Do

- **Test multiple variables at once:** You won't know what caused the change.
- **Change the test midway:** Let it finish.
- **Ignore segment differences:** A/B results may vary between new subscribers and longtime customers. Run separate tests for key segments.

**Action step:** Pick your next email send. Choose one element to test. Write two variants, and set a 48‑hour window. Then implement the winner for your next send. Build a habit of testing one thing at a time.
