# Why You Should Delete 20% of Your Email List Right Now

It sounds counterintuitive: you worked hard to grow that list, and now I'm telling you to delete contacts. But a smaller, engaged list will generate more revenue than a large, disinterested one.

## The Hidden Cost of Unengaged Subscribers

Mailbox providers like Gmail and Yahoo use engagement as a primary signal for inbox placement. If a large portion of your list never opens or clicks, your overall sender reputation drops. That means your emails start landing in spam—even for the subscribers who *are* engaged.

## Step 1: Define Unengaged

Create segments based on engagement windows:
- **Engaged:** Opened or clicked in the last 30 days.
- **Semi‑engaged:** Last engaged 31–90 days ago.
- **Unengaged:** 90+ days with no opens or clicks.

## Step 2: Run a Re‑engagement Campaign

Before deleting, give unengaged subscribers one last chance. Send a simple, honest email: “We've noticed you haven't opened our emails in a while. We'd like to keep you, but we don't want to clutter your inbox. Click here to stay, or we'll remove you in 7 days.” This often reactivates 5–10% of contacts.

## Step 3: Cut the Rest

After the grace period, delete or suppress everyone who didn't respond. Yes, delete. It frees up mental space and improves your metrics overnight. Your open rates will jump, your click rates will improve, and your deliverability will recover.

## Step 4: Plan for Ongoing List Hygiene

Make list cleaning a quarterly habit. Automate suppression of hard bounces and segment unengaged contacts automatically. A healthy list isn't the biggest one—it's the most responsive one.

**One powerful mindset shift:** Your list is not a museum of everyone who ever showed interest. It's a room full of people who want to hear from you. Treat it that way.
